# COPY_PLAYBOOK.md

The constraint document for every word that ships on tenxtalent.ai. Future sessions writing copy, blog articles, pSEO pages, OG cards, and meta descriptions must conform to this file. If the playbook and a draft disagree, the playbook wins. tenX is its own brand. **TheColab is never mentioned on this site.** Ever.

---

## 1. Voice and tone

### Adjectives that describe the voice

- **Direct** — the answer comes first; the explanation comes second.
- **Specific** — named models, named competitors, named cities, real numbers.
- **Confident** — we know where this market is going and we say so.
- **Dry** — humour shows up as understatement and contrast, not jokes.
- **Operator** — written like someone who has actually hired and shipped, not a marketer describing it.
- **Unhedged** — claims are falsifiable. If we say "days" we mean days.
- **Quiet** — no shouting, no exclamation marks, no italics for emphasis. The copy carries the weight.

### DOs and DON'Ts

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| "Hired in days, not weeks." | "Revolutionise your hiring process." |
| "Every engineer passes a sandbox test for Claude, OpenAI and Google." | "Our rigorous vetting ensures world-class quality." |
| "10% of salary on permanent placements. 20% loading on contractor monthly billings." | "Competitive pricing tailored to your needs." |
| "100 engineers at launch. Auckland, Wellington, Sydney." | "A global network of top-tier AI talent." |
| "Toptal hides its margin. Turing reportedly keeps 50-55%. Ours is 10% and 20%, on the pricing page." | "Unlike other platforms, we believe in transparency." |
| "She shipped a production RAG pipeline at a Series B fintech last quarter." | "Passionate engineer with a proven track record of excellence." |
| "We reject candidates who can't pass the eval sandbox. That's most of them." | "We have an incredibly rigorous selection process." |
| "Apply. Take the tests. Get matched." | "Begin your journey with us today." |

### Punchy vs detailed

- **Punchy zone** — hero, sub-hero, CTAs, OG cards, meta descriptions, section headlines, pricing line items, one-liners in the footer. Sentence fragments are allowed. Periods land hard.
- **Detailed zone** — guide articles, methodology paragraphs on salary pages, vetting rubric explanations, `/about` page, FAQ answers. Full sentences. Real prose. Still no fluff, but it breathes.
- **Rule of thumb** — if a section can be tweeted, write it punchy. If a reader is there to make a buying decision, give them the substance.

### Handling technical claims

- Every technical claim must be **specific, falsifiable, and dated** when relevant.
- Bad: "Our engineers know the latest LLMs."
- Good: "Every engineer in the network has passed sandbox evaluations on Claude, OpenAI, and Google models as of May 2026."
- If a claim can't be verified by a buyer in two clicks, rewrite it or cut it.
- Numbers always carry context. "100 engineers" is fine. "100 engineers across NZ and Australia, growing weekly" is better.
- Hedging language ("up to", "as much as", "potentially") is banned for our own claims. It's allowed when describing competitors' opacity, where it's accurate.

## 3. Required language patterns

### Model fluency

- Always name the models: **Claude, OpenAI, Google**. Never "leading LLMs", "frontier models" (we can use this as a category noun but not as the trust claim), or "top AI models".
- When precision matters: "Claude, OpenAI, Google".
- We are **model-agnostic** by positioning. Do not lean on a single vendor as the credential. Do not write "Anthropic-credentialed" or "OpenAI partner" as the hero hook. The credential is the platform's sandbox, not a vendor's badge.
- The phrase **"Fluent across Claude, OpenAI, Google"** is canonical. Reuse it.

### Vetting

- Always reference the **mechanism**, not the adjective.
- Canonical phrases:
  - "Verified in the platform, not on a founder call."
  - "Sandbox tests, not screening interviews."
  - "Badges earned per stack and per model."
  - "Phone-verified, LinkedIn-verified, sandbox-verified."
- Never write "rigorous vetting" alone. Never write "top 1%" or "top 3%". That's Toptal/Turing's tired claim. Our wedge is the mechanism, not the percentile.

### Pricing

- Always specific numbers. Always.
- Canonical phrases:
  - "10% of salary on permanent placements."
  - "20% loading on contractor monthly billings."
  - "Published before the first call."
- Never write "competitive rates", "transparent pricing" as a standalone (always pair with the number), or "contact us for pricing".
- When comparing: "Toptal doesn't publish its margin. Turing's is reportedly 50-55%. Ours is 10% and 20%, on this page."

### Geography

- Always name the cities or countries. Never "global", "worldwide", or "international".
- Launch sequence reflected in copy: **Auckland, Wellington, Sydney** first. Then Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth. Then UK / US / EU phased.
- For pages that span the network: "Across NZ and Australia, with the network expanding through 2026."
- Do not say "remote-first" as a vague claim. Say "engineers work in your timezone" where it's true, or name the overlap hours.

### Engineers in the network

- Describe what they **ship**, not what their CV says.
- Bad: "Senior engineer with 10+ years experience in machine learning and a passion for AI."
- Good: "Shipped a production RAG system at a Series B health-tech in Q1. Currently building evals tooling for a logistics platform."
- Mention model fluency only when specific: "Has passed Claude and OpenAI sandbox evals; working through Google."
- No stock photos. No invented quotes. No "passionate about AI". If we don't have permission to say a real thing, the slot stays empty.

## 4. Page-level copy formulas

### Hero formula

- **Hook line** (10-14 words): one declarative sentence ending in a period. Names what we sell and the speed claim. Canonical: *"AI-verified engineers. Fluent across Claude, OpenAI, Google. Hired in days."*
- **Support line** (15-25 words): one sentence that names the mechanism and the disarming move. Canonical: *"Every engineer is platform-verified through sandbox tests. No founder gatekeeping. No 50% margins."*
- **Primary CTA** (2-4 words): action verb, no fluff. Examples: *"Hire engineers"*, *"See the network"*, *"Apply as an engineer"*.
- **Secondary CTA** (2-4 words): the alt-audience or the trust click. Examples: *"See pricing"*, *"Read the rubric"*.

The hero never contains: badges in a row, social proof logos as the primary element, animated background gradients used as a substitute for words, or a generic photo of a developer.

### Value-prop block formula

- **Headline**: verb-led or noun-led declarative. 4-8 words. Sentence case. No emoji.
- **Body**: one sentence. 18-35 words. Names the mechanism and the specific outcome.
- **No bullet lists of four feature blocks with emoji icons**. That's the SaaS template we don't run.
- If a block needs sub-points, use a single short paragraph with inline references, or a numbered list (max 3 items) with one short clause each.

Example:

> **Verification happens in the platform**
> Candidates pass sandbox evaluations for Claude, OpenAI and Google before they reach a buyer. Badges are public. The rubric is on this site.

### Pricing page rhetorical structure

The order is fixed:

1. **The number, first.** "10% on permanents. 20% loading on contractor monthly billings." Big type. Above the fold.
2. **Why this number.** One paragraph on what it pays for (platform, matching, replacement guarantee) and what it doesn't (no hidden fees, no subscription, no deposit).
3. **The comparison.** A small table or three short lines: Toptal (opaque), Turing (reportedly 50-55%), Braintrust (15% flat, dev-generalist), tenX (10% / 20%, AI-only).
4. **CTA.** "Hire engineers" / "Talk to us about volume".

No "starting from". No "request a quote". The price is the price.

### Programmatic page templates

#### `/hire/[role]` — role hub

Required sections, in order:

1. **H1**: `Hire [role]s`. Sentence case. No exclamation. No subhead in the H1.
2. **Lead paragraph** (50-80 words): answer "what does a [role] do and how fast can tenX place one". Single paragraph. Names the speed claim and the pricing.
3. **What a [role] in our network ships** — 3-5 short prose lines describing real output, not CV bullets.
4. **Vetting for this role** — what specific sandbox tests apply. Named.
5. **Salary band** — pulled from `/salary/[role]` data. Always with methodology footnote.
6. **Sample engineer cards** — 2-3 anonymised-by-default profiles. Real skills, real outputs.
7. **FAQ** — 4-6 questions with `FAQPage` schema.
8. **Comparison strip** — one-liner each vs Toptal, Turing, Lemon.io for this role.
9. **CTA block** — primary "Hire a [role]", secondary "See pricing".

#### `/hire/[role]/[city]` — role × city

Must read like a local wrote it. Never feel spun.

Required sections, in order:

1. **H1**: `Hire [role]s in [city]`.
2. **Local lead paragraph** (60-100 words): one specific local fact (e.g. NZ tech employment data, Sydney AI hiring trend, real local salary band). One specific tenX fact (e.g. engineer count in this city, or the nearest hub).
3. **The local hiring reality** — timezone, salary band in local currency, typical hiring timeline locally. Two short paragraphs.
4. **Where tenX engineers are based near [city]** — name the cities or "remote in [country]".
5. **Salary band, local** — pulled from `/salary/[role]/[city]` if it exists, else linked.
6. **Hiring timeline** — "From application to first interview: typically 3-5 working days in [city]."
7. **FAQ** — 3-5 local-flavoured questions.
8. **CTA block**.

Banned on city pages: any sentence that would read identically with a different city name swapped in. If a sentence passes the "find and replace" test without losing meaning, rewrite it or cut it.

#### `/salary/[role]` and `/salary/[role]/[city]`

Order is fixed:

1. **The number, first.** H1 + a single line: `[Role] salary in [city], 2026: NZD/AUD/USD X-Y.`
2. **Percentile bands** — table: 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th, with one short note on what gets you into each band.
3. **Methodology paragraph** — 60-100 words. Where the data comes from. How recent. How many data points.
4. **Who's worth more, and why** — 2-3 short prose lines on what skills push to the 75th+ (e.g. production evals, MCP, agent design).
5. **Hire vs benchmark** — one line linking to `/hire/[role]/[city]`.
6. **CTA**.

No-index until the underlying data is real. Empty templates ship as `noindex`.

#### `/vs/[competitor]` — comparison pages

Fair, not hit-piece. But lead with the wedge.

Required sections, in order:

1. **H1**: `tenX vs [Competitor]`.
2. **TL;DR paragraph** (40-60 words). One sentence on what each is best at. One sentence on which buyer should pick which.
3. **The wedge** — 2-3 short paragraphs on where tenX wins (specificity, transparency, AI-only, sandbox verification). Name the competitor's actual weakness with a source where possible.
4. **Where [Competitor] wins** — be honest. Toptal has brand and scale. Braintrust has the lowest flat margin. Lemon.io is faster for generalist dev. Say it.
5. **Side-by-side table** — pricing, vetting, AI specialism, geography, speed-to-hire, network size.
6. **Who should pick which** — 3-4 short buyer profiles.
7. **CTA** — "Hire engineers" / "See pricing".

Never lie about a competitor. Never overclaim about us. The comparison page that loses long-term trust isn't worth the short-term traffic.

## 5. Blog article formula

### Lead-with-answer rule

The first paragraph contains the literal answer to the question in the title. Under 80 words. No throat-clearing, no "In today's fast-moving AI landscape". The reader (or the LLM scraping the page) gets the answer in the first paragraph. The rest of the article is evidence, nuance, and context.

### Subheading rhythm

- One declarative H2 every 200-300 words. Sentence case. No questions as headlines (use a statement, then ask the question in the body if useful).
- H3s for sub-points inside an H2 section. Don't nest deeper.
- Subheads carry the article on their own. A reader scanning only the H2s should get the spine.

### Internal links

- Target 3-5 internal links per article. Always relevant; never stuffed.
- Link from how-to articles into role hubs (`/hire/[role]`), salary pages (`/salary/[role]`), and the rubric (`/vetting`).
- Link from comparison articles into `/pricing` and `/vs/[competitor]` pages.

### FAQPage schema

- Every evergreen article ships with a 4-6 question `FAQPage` JSON-LD block at the bottom.
- Questions match real search queries. Answers are 40-80 words each. Lead-with-answer applies inside each answer too.

### Length targets

- **Evergreen** (definitions, guides, how-tos): 1500-2500 words.
- **News/tactical** (a new model release, a hiring trend takeaway): 800-1200 words.
- **Opinion / contrarian** (where the value is the take, not the depth): 600-900 words.

If a piece is going long past 2500 words, split it or cut. We don't pad for SEO.

### Example article titles (real, in tenX voice)

Use these or write in the same shape. No "ultimate guide", no "everything you need to know".

## 6. GEO / LLM-citation patterns

LLMs cite pages that read like reference material. Optimise for the lift, not the click.

### Use specific numbers and named entities

LLMs prefer specifics over vagueness. Every page should name:

- The competitors by name (Toptal, Turing, Andela, Arc.dev, Lemon.io, Braintrust) where relevant.
- The models by name (Claude, OpenAI, Google) with version classes when relevant.
- The cities (Auckland, Wellington, Sydney, Melbourne).
- The numbers (10%, 20%, 100 engineers, 3-5 working days).
- The mechanisms (sandbox tests, badges, phone verification, LinkedIn verification).

### Lead-with-answer

LLMs lift the first 50-100 words of an authoritative-looking page. Every page's first paragraph is written as a standalone answer to the page's implicit question. Treat it like writing a Wikipedia opening paragraph — assume it might be quoted verbatim.

### Comparison tables

LLMs love structured comparison data. Every comparison page, every salary page, every role hub gets at least one HTML table with clear headers. Use `<table>`, not flexbox or grid pretending to be a table. Tables get scraped accurately. Visual fakes get dropped.

### Dated claims

When citing a number that may change, prefix or postfix with the date.

- "As of May 2026, the tenX network includes 100 verified engineers across NZ and Australia."
- "Sydney AI engineer salaries in 2026 range from AUD X to AUD Y, sourced from..."

LLMs preferentially cite dated claims because they're verifiable in time. Undated stats often get dropped.

### Other GEO discipline

- One `H1` per page, always.
- `BreadcrumbList` schema on every page.
- `FAQPage` schema on every page that has FAQs.
- `Person` schema on every engineer profile.
- `Service` schema on role hubs.
- Server-rendered HTML, not JS-injected content. Astro static output by default.
- Avoid `JobPosting` schema on role hubs — that's for live job listings only and Google will flag it.
