The short answer: use a broad global network when you need general developer coverage, use an AI-specific verified network when the work depends on practical AI skill across providers, evaluation, RAG, agents, MLOps, or code-with-AI. tenX is built for the second case.

Toptal and Turing are known brands in technical hiring. They can be useful if the role is broad, the geography is flexible, and the buyer accepts a marketplace-style process. The tradeoff is that their pricing and margin structures are not as transparent as a buyer might want, and their networks are not designed around AI-specific assessment badges as the primary trust signal.

tenX starts from a narrower premise. AI engineering is now specific enough to deserve its own verification layer. A candidate saying they have used LLMs is not sufficient. The buyer needs to know whether the engineer can choose between Claude, OpenAI, and Google, build retrieval with citations, design evals, control cost, use AI coding tools safely, and operate inside product constraints.

## Pricing visibility

The most immediate difference is pricing visibility. tenX publishes its fees: <a href={href("/pricing")}>10% of first-year salary for permanent placements and 20% loading on monthly contractor billings</a>. The buyer can model the cost before speaking to anyone.

That is not how many global talent networks work. Toptal publishes parts of its commercial model, including deposits and platform subscription details in its public FAQ, but the role-specific all-in rates and platform economics are not the same as a clean margin line. Turing is generally quote-based. Independent market commentary has described Turing contractor margins as rumoured around 50-55%, but those figures should be treated as rumours, not official disclosures.

The important point is not to litigate a competitor's exact spread. The important point is that tenX removes the ambiguity. If the engineer bills a monthly amount, the contractor loading is 20%. If you hire permanently, the placement fee is 10% of first-year salary.

## Verification depth

AI engineering hiring fails when vetting stops at resume claims. A candidate can list LangChain, OpenAI, Claude, agents, vector databases, and automation tooling without proving they can ship a reliable system.

tenX uses platform-based skills assessments. Engineers earn badges for tests passed across Claude fluency, OpenAI fluency, Google fluency, prompt engineering, RAG, agent design, evals, MLOps, and code-with-AI. The <a href={href("/vetting")}>full vetting criteria</a> — what each test measures, pass criteria, and retry policy — is published. That gives the network a structured trust layer. More badges increase match priority, so candidates have a reason to keep proving relevant skills.

Broad networks may have strong engineers, but the buyer still needs to map general vetting to AI-specific work. With tenX, the badge system is the product. It is designed for the hiring questions that matter now: can this engineer compare models, manage uncertainty, design evaluation, and make the workflow safe enough to use?

## Geography and launch focus

Toptal and Turing are global by default. That is useful if you want maximum geographic reach. tenX is intentionally staged: New Zealand first, Australia second, then US, UK, and EU later.

That narrower geography is a feature for the launch market. NZ companies often need people who understand local operating constraints, time zones, compensation expectations, and the difference between a flashy AI demo and a system a small team can maintain. AUS expansion follows in June 2026, with Sydney and Melbourne already scaffolded in the network data.

If you need a global timezone spread immediately, a broad network may be the right tool. If you need a verified NZ-first AI engineering shortlist, tenX is built closer to the buyer's reality.

## Candidate experience

The candidate side matters because the best AI engineers do not want to repeat unstructured screening calls. They want their proof to compound.

tenX launches with candidates first. Engineers <a href={href("/apply")}>apply</a>, take practical tests, earn badges, and then receive higher match priority when employer access opens. That sequence matters. It means the employer launch starts with evidence already in the system rather than a cold marketplace.

It also removes manual screening from the trust mechanism. The platform does the initial verification work, so the network can scale without making every candidate repeat the same interview.

## When Toptal or Turing can still make sense

This comparison is not a claim that broad networks are useless. They can make sense when the role is a general software build, when AI is incidental, when the buyer wants global coverage immediately, or when an internal team already has strong AI vetting and only needs sourcing volume.

The tradeoff appears when the work is actually AI engineering. If the project needs retrieval quality, model routing, agent design, evaluation, observability, or safe AI coding workflows, generic screening leaves too much for the employer to verify. The hiring process either slows down or accepts risk.
